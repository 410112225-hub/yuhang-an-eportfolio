05 Reflective Learning

Reflective Learning and Peer Feedback

These reflective posts show how my learning has developed through business professionalism, work experience and peer feedback.

Post 1

Digital Professional Identity

One important skill I developed in this course is understanding how to present a professional digital identity. Before this assessment, I thought an online profile was only a place to list education and work experience. Now I understand that an ePortfolio and LinkedIn profile also communicate personality, values, goals and readiness for the workplace. A professional digital identity needs clear language, accurate information and evidence that supports the claims I make about myself.

This skill is useful because employers and professional contacts may search for me before meeting me. If my online profile is incomplete or unclear, it can create a weak first impression. By organising my background, CV, project work and reflections in one place, I can show a more complete picture of my journey from bakery operations in China to business study in New Zealand. In the real world, I can use this skill when applying for part-time work, internships or future business opportunities. It will also help me build trust with customers and partners when I develop my own bakery brand.

Creating the portfolio also made me think more carefully about consistency. My CV, LinkedIn profile and website should describe my experience in the same honest and professional way. I therefore checked that my dates, responsibilities and career direction were clear across each section. I also selected evidence that supports my claims instead of making the page only decorative. From now on, I will review my digital profile at the end of every study term, add completed projects or new skills, and remove information that is no longer current. This regular review will help me maintain an accurate professional identity and show gradual development rather than creating an online profile only when I need to apply for a job.

Post 2

Customer Communication and Service Recovery

Through my Air New Zealand project, I learned that customer communication is not only about giving information. It is also about reducing uncertainty, showing respect and helping customers feel that the business is taking responsibility. When a service problem happens, customers want timely updates, clear choices and sincere support. Research used in my project also showed that the quality of service recovery influences customer satisfaction and loyalty (Nguyen Ngoc Hien et al., 2024; Tran, 2024). If communication is slow or confusing, the problem can become much bigger than the original disruption.

This learning is very useful for my future bakery career. In a bakery store, problems can happen with product quality, waiting time, stock shortage or customer expectations. I learned that staff should acknowledge the issue, explain the solution clearly and follow up when needed. In the real world, I can apply this by creating simple service recovery steps for a bakery team: listen carefully, apologise when appropriate, offer a fair solution, and record the problem so it does not repeat. Good communication can turn a negative moment into a chance to rebuild customer trust.

The project also showed me that the timing and channel of communication matter. A customer should not have to search several places for basic information while a problem is continuing. For a future bakery, I would decide in advance who updates customers, what information can be shared, and when an issue should be escalated to a manager. For example, if an important product is unavailable, staff could explain the reason, suggest a suitable alternative and update online information quickly. I would also review repeated complaints each month to identify patterns. This approach connects respectful communication with operational improvement, because the business learns from the problem instead of treating every complaint as an isolated event.

Post 3

Teamwork and Store Operations

My work experience in a family chain bakery store taught me that teamwork is built through daily habits. In a busy store, staff need to understand their responsibilities, communicate changes quickly and support each other during peak times. A small delay in stock checking, product display or customer service can affect the whole team. This course helped me connect those practical experiences with professional business concepts such as coordination, responsibility and continuous improvement.

The skill I strengthened is seeing teamwork from a management perspective. I now pay more attention to how tasks are assigned, how communication flows and how a leader can create a calm working environment. This is useful because my future goal is not only to work in a bakery, but to manage and grow a bakery brand. In the real world, I can apply this by preparing clear staff duties, simple checklists and respectful communication standards. A good team can make customers feel welcome and help the business operate more consistently.

I can apply this learning by using a short opening checklist before each shift and a brief handover at the end. The checklist could cover stock, display, special orders, cleaning tasks and the main responsibilities for each staff member. The handover could record problems that the next shift needs to know. I would also encourage staff to ask for help early, because silence can allow a small operational issue to affect customers. As a future leader, I want to give clear direction while remaining approachable. I will practise this in group assignments by confirming responsibilities, checking progress respectfully and inviting quieter members to contribute. These actions will help me develop teamwork as a repeatable management practice, not only as a personal attitude.

Post 4

Strategic Thinking

Another skill I developed is strategic thinking. When I first worked on business assignments, I focused mainly on describing problems. The Air New Zealand project helped me move beyond description and start thinking about causes, objectives, actions and measurement. Using tools such as SWOT analysis, SMART objectives and KPIs made my ideas more structured and practical (KPI.org, 2014; Sammut-Bonnici & Galea, 2015). I learned that a strategy should not be only a general suggestion; it should connect business problems with clear actions and evidence.

This skill is useful for my future because bakery businesses also need strategy. A store cannot rely only on good products. It needs positioning, customer understanding, pricing decisions, staff training, online promotion and quality control. In the real world, I can apply strategic thinking when planning new products, choosing a target market or improving customer experience. For example, if sales decrease, I should not only guess the reason. I should collect information, identify possible causes, set an improvement goal and measure whether the action works.

A practical example would be testing a new seasonal product. I could first identify the target customer and expected benefit, then set a SMART objective for sales or customer feedback during a four-week trial. Measures might include units sold, waste, repeat purchases and short customer comments. At the end of the trial, I would compare the results with the objective and decide whether to continue, change or stop the product. This process reduces decisions based only on personal preference. In future assignments and workplace situations, I will use the same sequence: define the problem, gather relevant evidence, choose an action, set a measure and review the outcome. This gives my ideas a clearer business purpose.

Post 5

Presentation Confidence

Presentation is an important professional skill that I am still developing. Because English is not my first language, I sometimes feel nervous when speaking in front of others. Preparing this ePortfolio presentation helped me practise how to introduce myself, explain my work experience and connect my past learning with my future career goals. I learned that confidence does not only come from language ability. It also comes from preparation, structure and knowing the message I want the audience to remember.

This skill is useful because professional opportunities often require speaking clearly, whether in interviews, meetings, customer conversations or business presentations. In the real world, I can apply this by preparing key points before meetings, practising short introductions and using visual evidence to support what I say. For my future bakery brand, presentation skills will help me communicate with staff, suppliers, partners and customers. My next step is to practise regularly, ask for feedback and become more comfortable speaking in professional English.

To make this improvement measurable, I will record one five-minute presentation every two weeks during 2026. After each recording, I will review my pronunciation, speed, eye contact and structure, and write down one change for the next practice. I will also ask a classmate or tutor for feedback at least once each month. When I prepare for an interview or presentation, I will use a simple beginning, middle and conclusion so the audience can follow my message. I do not expect every sentence to be perfect. My goal is to communicate the main idea accurately, respond calmly to questions and show professional preparation. Regular practice should make confidence a result of experience rather than something I wait to feel.

Peer Evaluation

Learning From Feedback

Peer evaluation helped me see my work from another person's point of view. Feedback from classmates and group discussions showed me that my ideas are stronger when I organise them clearly and explain the connection between evidence and goals. I also learned that I should speak with more confidence, use simpler sentences when presenting, and make sure each section has a clear purpose. This feedback was useful because it gave me practical actions instead of only general comments.

I responded to this feedback by improving the structure of my ePortfolio and presentation. I separated my background, project work, professional development and reflections so that the audience can follow my story more easily. I also added evidence from my bakery experience and study project to make my claims more believable. In the real world, peer feedback is important because professional growth is not only based on self-opinion. Managers, colleagues and customers can all provide information that helps me improve. I will continue to ask for feedback and use it to develop my communication, teamwork and leadership skills.

The feedback also changed how I prepare. Instead of including every detail, I now identify the main message for each section and choose evidence that supports it. For presentations, I practise shorter sentences and clearer transitions. My next step is to keep a feedback log after group tasks and presentations, recording the feedback, action and result. This will help me apply advice over time and view feedback as useful information for improving my professional performance.
